## Why estimation matters

Strong system design answers are quantitative enough to feel real. Estimation helps you reject architectures that obviously do not fit the workload.

## A simple flow

1. Estimate daily active users.
2. Convert daily behavior into requests per second.
3. Estimate read-to-write ratios.
4. Calculate storage growth over time.
5. Add a safety margin.

## Example: short-link service

Suppose the product receives **10 million new links per month** and each redirect is read **100 times** on average.

- New writes per second stay modest.
- Redirect reads dominate traffic.
- A cache becomes valuable much earlier than a sharded write path.

<InterviewTip title="Narrate assumptions clearly">
Interviewers care less about exact numbers than whether your assumptions are reasonable, explicit, and internally consistent.
</InterviewTip>

## Common anchors to memorize

- `1 day = 86,400 seconds`
- `1 KB = 10^3 bytes` for quick approximation
- `1 million / day` is about `12 requests / second`
- Traffic peaks are often `2x` to `5x` the average

## What this unlocks

Once you can size the system, conversations about caching, replication, rate limiting, queues, and storage all become much more concrete.
